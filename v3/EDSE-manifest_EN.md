The Evidence-Driven Software Evolution Manifesto (**EDSE**)

Software development is not merely about writing code. It is about understanding problems, asking the right questions, evaluating alternatives, and making decisions that will shape a system for years to come.

Yet many of the most important decisions in a project are eventually lost in meetings, informal conversations, or short-lived messages. As a result, a system's evolution often depends more on the memory of its contributors than on the knowledge accumulated by the project itself.

Evidence-Driven Software Evolution (**EDSE**) proposes a different way to evolve software systems.

We believe that every significant change should begin with an explicit question, be supported by verifiable evidence, and be documented in such a way that anyone can understand not only what was decided, but also why that decision was made.

Documentation is no longer something produced after development is complete. It becomes an integral part of the engineering process itself.

Architecture no longer evolves through isolated opinions, but through research, validation, and continuous learning.

The knowledge generated throughout a project is a strategic asset and deserves the same level of discipline applied to writing code.

Therefore, we declare the following principles.

The Ten Principles of **EDSE** ## Every significant decision must be traceable.

Architectural, functional, and technical decisions should always be linked to the evidence that motivated them and to the outcomes they produced.

## Questions come before solutions.

Solutions should never be implemented before the underlying problem has been fully understood.

Every investigation begins with questions—not with code.

## Evidence takes precedence over intuition.

Experience is valuable, but critical decisions should be supported by analysis, experimentation, validation, or other forms of verifiable evidence.

## Every decision must preserve its context.

Recording the conclusion is not enough.

The problem, the alternatives considered, and the reasoning behind the chosen solution should also be preserved.

## Architecture evolves through controlled iterations.

Software systems should not evolve through arbitrary changes.

Each iteration is an opportunity to learn, validate assumptions, and reduce uncertainty.

## Documentation is part of development.

Documentation is not an afterthought.

It is a mechanism for creating, validating, and sharing knowledge throughout the evolution of a system.

## Knowledge must outlive the team.

No project should depend solely on the people who originally made its decisions.

Knowledge should remain accessible to everyone who continues its evolution.

## Every change should improve understanding.

A successful change does more than modify the software.

It also improves the team's understanding of the system itself.

## Validation never ends.

Every decision remains open to new evidence.

Continuous improvement is the natural consequence of continuous learning.

## Software evolution is a research process.

Every project is an opportunity to generate reusable knowledge.

Software engineering is not only about building systems—it is also about learning how to build better ones.

**EDSE** Commitments

By adopting this methodology, practitioners commit to:

Asking questions before proposing solutions. Clearly distinguishing evidence, hypotheses, and opinions. Recording significant decisions together with their context. Maintaining traceability between research, decisions, and implementation. Continuously validating outcomes. Treating project knowledge as a long-term strategic asset. Definition

Evidence-Driven Software Evolution (**EDSE**) is a software engineering methodology that guides the continuous evolution of software systems through structured research, verifiable evidence, traceable decision-making, and living documentation, ensuring that the knowledge generated throughout a project's lifecycle remains accessible, reusable, and continuously verifiable.

### New Knowledge

This shift represents the essence of **EDSE**.

Rather than focusing primarily on managing work, **EDSE** focuses on managing the knowledge that enables better decisions.

Every implementation is the result of explicit reasoning.

Every decision has a documented rationale.

Every iteration increases the project's understanding.

Every project becomes an opportunity not only to build software, but also to produce reusable engineering knowledge.
